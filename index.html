01
Every state, every year
This chart shows 50 US states across 14 years of Census data. Each colored line is one state. Each vertical axis is one year from 2010 to 2024. Follow a line left to right to see how its commute time changed over the years.
02
A decade of rising commutes
From 2010 to 2019, commute times went up in almost every state, every single year. Every region. The lines all move upward together.
10 straight years of growth
Did you know?
By 2019, about 9% of US workers were spending 60 minutes or more commuting each way, every workday. That is over 14 million people.
03
The Northeast bears the most
The red lines, which are Northeast states, sit at the top on almost every axis. States like New York, New Jersey, and Maryland are consistently above 30 minutes average.
Northeast: 28+ min avg
New York fact
In 2024, 17.2% of New York workers commuted 60 or more minutes each way. That is about 1 in 6 workers spending over 2 hours a day just getting to and from work.
04
COVID changed everything for a bit
In 2021, every single line drops at that axis. California fell from 30.7 to 27.6 minutes. New York dropped from 33 to 30. Remote work cut commutes for the first time in 10 years.
2021: First drop in 10 years
The COVID numbers
The average Northeast commute dropped from 28.4 min in 2019 to 26.3 min in 2021. That works out to about 35 extra hours a year that workers got back just by staying home a few days a week.
05
Then it was taken back
By 2022 and 2024, the lines went right back up. California climbed from 27.6 back to 29.7. New York rebounded to 33 minutes. Once offices reopened, commutes came back too.
2024: Back to peak levels
California fact
California's average commute is 29.7 minutes in 2024, but 12.1% of workers there commute 60 or more minutes each way. The average hides a big group of people with really long drives, especially around LA and the Bay Area.
